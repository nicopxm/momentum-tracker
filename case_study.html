01 — Problem

By the time you see it,
it's already too late

Non-professional crypto traders have no real-time infrastructure to detect early momentum before it becomes mainstream. The tools that exist are either priced for institutions or focused on large caps that don't move the way mid and small cap coins do.

The Timing Problem
By the time a pump is visible on a chart, the best entry is gone. Volume confirmation — the signal most tools wait for — typically arrives after 15–30% of the move has already happened. You're buying the momentum, not getting ahead of it.
The Coverage Problem
Coinbase lists 231+ active USD pairs. Manually monitoring even 20 of them for early signals is impossible in real time. The best moves — BOBBOB, FIDA, FIGHT, KTA — happened on coins most traders weren't watching at all.
The Noise Problem
Simple volume spike alerts generate enormous false positive rates. Without multi-signal confirmation — volume ratio, acceleration stages, liquidity filters, RSI — every alert feels like a potential trade. Alert fatigue kills execution discipline.
The Exit Problem
Even when you catch the right coin at the right time, no automated exit signal exists. BILL-USD peaked at +63% from entry and is now -41%. UP-USD peaked at +103% and is now below entry. Without TP/SL automation, gains evaporate.
02 — Solution

A multi-signal momentum
engine with automated exits

Momentum fuses six independent signal types into a single detection pipeline, applies dynamic thresholds based on current market context, and manages positions automatically from entry through exit.

L1
Price Momentum Detection
Four timeframes (5min, 15min, 30min, 1hr) monitored simultaneously. L1 fires on price threshold breach. Filters: low liquidity (<$50k daily volume), direction check, 24hr context. 231 coins × 4 configs every 7 minutes.
L2
Volume Confirmation (Standard L2)
Upgrades L1 when volume ratio exceeds timeframe threshold (1.5x–3.0x). Requires positive price change, positive 24hr change, sufficient liquidity. L2 is the primary alert signal — the system only sends Telegram alerts on confirmed L2 or above.
DL2
Dynamic L2 — Early Detection (Option #2)
The core innovation. When a coin already has strong 24hr momentum (≥15%), five safeguards evaluate whether to lower the volume bar: (1) Fresh leg test — price rising in last 30min, (2) Peak proximity — >8% below peak, (3) Acceleration required — ≥1 confirmed stage, (4) Volume trend — 2 of 3 candles rising, (5) Separate alert type — labeled UNCONFIRMED so traders know the risk. Fires as EARLY L2 — does not pollute the standard L2 signal.
HH
HH/HL Multi-Leg Pattern Detection
Reads from daily_snapshots (one row per coin per day). Detects Higher Highs and Higher Lows — the structural anatomy of a healthy multi-day uptrend. 3% tolerance on lows to filter wick noise. Returns CONFIRMED (HH+HL) or PRE_BREAKOUT (HL only — best entry). Calculates last_hl_price as natural support/entry level.
GR
Slow Grinder Detection
Three-tier system (Fast/Mid/Slow) based on daily compounded rate from daily_snapshots. Approach B uptrend check: last 25% price average must be 5%+ above first 25%. Capped mean for avg_vol (cap at 10x) prevents single volume spikes from disqualifying genuine grinders. Slow grinders exempt from weak signal time stops.
TP
Automated TP/SL Position Management
Five exit types: TP1 (+20% partial, size varies by profile), trailing stop (wick-filtered using 1-min closes not highs), dynamic hard stop (-8% standard / -12% grinders), breakeven trigger (peak ever +12% moves floor to entry), weak signal exit (2hr no traction — grinders exempt). Boolean flags prevent re-firing. Position resets on new L2.

Wick-Filtered Trailing Stop

The critical engineering decision in the TP/SL system. Low-cap momentum coins frequently generate "rogue wicks" — a single market buy order into a thin order book creates a 1-minute candle spike that instantly reverts. Using df["high"] for the trailing stop would trigger false exits on these wicks.

Solution: trail on df["close"].iloc[-15:].max() — the highest confirmed 1-minute close, not the highest wick. Candle closes represent agreed-upon market value. Wicks represent temporary liquidity voids. The same wick-filtered close feeds peak_price in coin_state, ensuring the breakeven trigger activates correctly even when intra-cycle spikes occur between 7-minute polls.

05 — Reflections

What I learned building
a production trading system

What worked

The batch state cache was the single biggest architectural win. Replacing 231 individual per-coin SELECT queries with one batch read per cycle eliminated ~65MB/day of egress from state reads alone. Combined with conditional momentum history and explicit column selection on summary queries, total egress dropped from 16GB to 2.5GB/month — well within Supabase's free tier.

What worked

Mandatory code review cycles caught three critical silent failures: TA indicators written in an unreachable except block (weeks of null RSI), 194 coins with stale l2_fired_at timestamps permanently blocking re-entry alerts, and peak_price updating indefinitely on closed positions — creating phantom +500% gains that corrupted Hall of Fame data.

What I'd do differently

The egress architecture should have been designed from day one around a batch state cache. Retrofitting it after the first month cost significant engineering time and allowed the original account to exceed Supabase's free tier limits before the fix was deployed. Schema decisions are harder to change than logic decisions.

What I'd do differently

I'd build the TP/SL system in parallel with signal detection, not after it. BILL-USD peaked at +63% and gave it all back. Every day the system ran without automated exits was a day real gains were left on the table. Exit logic is not a feature — it's core infrastructure. The Hall of Fame auto-insert should have been wired from day one.

Unexpected insight

Silent failures are the hardest bugs to find. Indicators were calculated correctly every cycle — but written in an except block that never ran because the try always succeeded. Three weeks of null RSI values before the root cause was found. In production systems, always verify writes explicitly — log confirmation, not just the calculation.

Unexpected insight

The rogue wick problem in trailing stops is a first-principles engineering issue, not a trading one. Using candle highs for trailing stop calculation causes false exits on thin-order-book coins that spike and immediately revert. Trailing on 1-minute closes — now widened to a 15-candle lookback — catches more intra-cycle peaks without being fooled by temporary liquidity voids.
